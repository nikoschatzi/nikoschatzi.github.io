ACS712 Current Sensor — Characterisation and Experimental Validation

Components Hardware Testing

Overview

The ACS712 is an integrated Hall-effect current sensor used in motor drive setups to measure winding current. It is an attractive choice for embedded applications due to its low cost, reasonable accuracy, and adequate sensitivity. Current measurement is based on the Hall effect: a current-carrying conductor placed in a magnetic field produces a transverse voltage proportional to the current magnitude.

Available Versions

Three variants of the ACS712 are available, each optimised for a different current range:

The ±5 A variant (ACS712-05B) was selected because it covers the full operating current range of the motor under test and offers the highest sensitivity among the three — a desirable property for accurate closed-loop control. Operating temperature range is −40 °C to +85 °C.

Output Characteristic

The sensor output is a ratiometric DC voltage centred at 2.5 V at zero current:

Vout = 2.5 V + (185 mV/A) × I
Key behaviour: Output increases by 185 mV per ampere for positive current, and decreases by 185 mV per ampere for negative current. At I = 0 A, Vout = 2.5 V.

Experimental Validation

To verify the datasheet transfer function, measurements were taken using a wirewound power resistor (measured resistance: 3.3 Ω, rated power: 15 W) and a dual-channel oscilloscope.

Fig. 2 — Wirewound power resistor (15 W, 3.3 Ω)
Fig. 2. Wirewound resistor used as the test load.

The test circuit connected the ACS712 in series with the power resistor:

  • Ch1 — oscilloscope channel measuring the ACS712 output voltage
  • Ch2 — oscilloscope channel measuring the voltage across the power resistor
Fig. 3 — Test circuit schematic
Fig. 3. Wiring for comparing experimental measurements with datasheet values.

The actual current through the resistor is derived from Ch2 via Ohm's law. For example, if Ch2 reads 2.7 V across the 3.3 Ω resistor:

I = V / R = 2.7 V / 3.3 Ω ≈ 0.818 A

Maximum Current Constraint

To protect the resistor from exceeding its 15 W power rating, the maximum allowable current was calculated:

Imax = √(Pmax / R) = √(15 / 3.3) ≈ 2.13 A

A conservative operating limit of 1.85 A was chosen, keeping a safe margin below the thermal limit. At this current the dissipated power is:

P = I² × R = 1.85² × 3.3 ≈ 11.3 W

Discussion

As shown in the figure above, the experimental results closely follow the datasheet transfer function. However, a consistent negative offset of approximately −10 mV is observed across all measurement points. This is attributed to a unit-level offset of the specific ACS712 module used in the experiment.

Offset correction: A fixed offset of ~10 mV should be subtracted from all ACS712 readings when using this particular unit. In software this can be handled by calibrating the zero-current output voltage at startup and subtracting it from subsequent measurements.

Despite this small offset, the linearity and sensitivity of the sensor are in excellent agreement with the datasheet, confirming the ACS712-05B as a suitable current sense element for this motor drive application.
