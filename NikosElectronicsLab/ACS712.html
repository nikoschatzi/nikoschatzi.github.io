ACS712 Current Sensor — Characterisation and Experimental Validation

Components Hardware Testing

Overview

The ACS712 is an integrated Hall-effect current sensor used in motor drive setups to measure winding current. It is attractive for embedded applications due to its low cost, good accuracy, and adequate sensitivity. Current measurement relies on the Hall effect: a current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field produces a transverse voltage proportional to the current.

Fig. 1. ACS712 current sensor module.

The ±5 A variant (ACS712-05B) was selected: it covers the full operating current range of the test motor and offers the highest sensitivity — important for accurate closed-loop current control. Operating temperature range: −40 °C to +85 °C.

Output Characteristic

The sensor output is a ratiometric DC voltage centred at 2.5 V at zero current:

Vout = 2.5 V + (185 mV/A) × I
Key behaviour: Output increases by 185 mV per ampere for positive current, and decreases by 185 mV per ampere for negative current. At I = 0 A, Vout = 2.5 V.

Experimental Validation

To verify the datasheet transfer function, measurements were performed using a wirewound power resistor (measured: 3.3 Ω, rated: 15 W) and a dual-channel oscilloscope.

Fig. 2. Wirewound resistor (15 W, 3.3 Ω) used as test load.

The ACS712 was connected in series with the resistor. Two oscilloscope channels were used:

  • Ch1 — ACS712 output voltage
  • Ch2 — voltage across the power resistor (used to derive actual current via Ohm's law)
Fig. 3. Test circuit — ACS712 in series with power resistor, dual-channel oscilloscope measurement.

The actual current through the resistor is derived from the Ch2 reading via Ohm's law. For example, if Ch2 reads 2.7 V:

I = V / R = 2.7 V / 3.3 Ω ≈ 0.818 A

Maximum Current Limit

To avoid exceeding the 15 W resistor rating, the maximum allowable test current was bounded:

Imax = √(P / R) = √(15 / 3.3) ≈ 2.13 A

A conservative limit of 1.85 A was used, keeping safe margin below the thermal limit. Dissipated power at this point:

P = I² × R = 1.85² × 3.3 ≈ 11.3 W

Discussion

As shown in Fig. 4, the experimental results closely follow the datasheet transfer function across the full test range. A consistent negative offset of approximately −10 mV is observed at every measurement point, attributed to a unit-level offset of the specific module used.

Offset correction: A fixed offset of ~10 mV should be subtracted from ACS712 readings. In firmware, this is handled by sampling the output at zero current during initialisation and subtracting it from subsequent measurements — a standard calibration routine for Hall-effect current sensors.

Despite the small offset, the linearity and sensitivity are in excellent agreement with the datasheet, confirming the ACS712-05B as a suitable current sensing element for this motor drive application.
